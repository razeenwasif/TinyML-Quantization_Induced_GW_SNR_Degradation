The 2024 review by Cuoco et al. provides a comprehensive assessment of how Machine Learning (ML) is currently being integrated into the data analysis workflows of the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA (LVK) detector network. The paper categorizes the application of ML into several primary domains:

- **Noise Mitigation and Detector Characterization:** The authors highlight that current interferometers are severely impacted by non-stationary noise and transient glitches. ML algorithms—such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) operating on time-frequency spectrograms and Dictionary Learning—are heavily utilized to classify glitches and denoise the strain data. Tools like DeepClean and NonSENS have successfully demonstrated the ability to subtract persistent noise (like the 60 Hz power line) using deep neural networks.
    
- **Signal Detection (Modeled and Unmodeled):** For Compact Binary Coalescences (CBCs), supervised deep learning models are being deployed to approach the sensitivity of traditional matched-filtering pipelines while offering massive reductions in computational latency at inference time. ML is also being used to augment unmodeled burst searches, with ensemble methods like XGBoost acting as a penalty factor to distinguish signal events from background noise.
    
- **Source Interpretation and Parameter Estimation (PE):** Traditional Bayesian inference (like nested sampling) is identified as a major computational bottleneck taking days to weeks to run. ML techniques, specifically normalizing flows and Conditional Variational Auto-Encoders (CVAEs), are being implemented to accelerate PE to the order of seconds, which is a critical requirement for generating rapid alerts for multi-messenger astronomy.
    
- **Next-Generation and Space-Based Detectors:** The review notes that future detectors, including space-based missions like the Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA), will operate in a signal-dominated regime requiring the unentangling of overlapping signals. Deep neural networks are already being explored to handle these extreme-mass-ratio inspirals and complex data streams.
    
- **Current Limitations:** Despite the speed advantages at inference time, the authors explicitly note that ML algorithms act as "black boxes" lacking interpretability, and that their training phases require immense computational costs and GPU architectures.
